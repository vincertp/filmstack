# Builder Ethos

## The three questions before you write anything

Every project in filmstack starts with the same discipline. Before you open Final Draft, before you outline, before you pitch:

**1. What is the wound?**
Not the plot. The wound. The thing the protagonist cannot outrun regardless of what happens externally. Story is character. Character is wound. If you cannot name the wound in one sentence, you are not ready to write.

**2. Who loses if this doesn't get made?**
Not "who is the audience." Who specifically is worse off if this story doesn't exist in the world? If the answer is "nobody in particular," rethink the project.

**3. What is the smallest version of this that works?**
The full vision is a 3-season arc. The minimum viable version is a 90-minute film. The wedge is a 10-minute short that proves the world is worth living in. Start with the wedge.

---

## Story before structure

Structure is a tool for communicating story, not a substitute for it. Three-act structure, Save the Cat, Hero's Journey — these are maps. Maps describe territory; they don't create it.

When `/script-doctor` finds a problem in Act Two, the problem is almost never structural. It's almost always that the character stopped wanting something specific enough to drive action. Fix the want, and the structure fixes itself.

---

## Compression over coverage

A 90-minute film that says one thing clearly is worth ten 150-minute films that say many things vaguely. The best `/coverage` reports recommend compression, not expansion. When in doubt, cut.

---

## The comparable titles trap

`/market-comp` will give you comparable titles. Use them to communicate, not to create. "It's *Parasite* meets *Succession*" tells a buyer what drawer to put it in. It does not tell you what the film is. Never let comp titles define your creative choices.

---

## On AI and creative work

filmstack uses AI to do the analytical and logistical work that consumes creative time: coverage, breakdown, scheduling, compliance scanning. The creative decisions — what the film is about, what it costs emotionally, what it asks of its audience — remain entirely with you.

The goal is not to automate filmmaking. The goal is to give one person with a vision the analytical firepower that used to require a team.
